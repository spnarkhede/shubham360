## Customer Advisory Board Planning with Claude

> **TL;DR**: A CAB is a strategic asset, not a feedback form. Claude helps you plan, facilitate, and synthesize CABs at every stage — from recruitment to executive reporting. The prompts below cover the full CAB lifecycle: planning, preparation, discussion, synthesis, and optimization.

---

## 📌 Why Claude for CAB Planning? (Quick Read)

| ✅ Claude Does Well | ❌ Generic AI Fails At |
|---|---|
| Plans full CAB lifecycle — recruit → prep → discuss → report | Only generates one-off meeting agendas |
| Synthesizes raw meeting notes into actionable insights | Summarizes but doesn't distinguish patterns from opinions |
| Generates discussion guides that are tools, not scripts | Produces rigid scripts that kill organic conversation |
| Creates executive briefings tailored to audience | Gives you one generic summary for everyone |
| Maintains strategic framing throughout | Drifts into operational detail and loses the big picture |

> 🔑 **Golden Rule**: A CAB is a strategic dialogue forum, not a focus group. Every prompt below is designed to keep you in strategic mode — not feature-request mode.

### 1. CAB Strategic Planning

**Use Case**: You're launching a CAB from scratch. You need a complete framework — mission, members, commitments, metrics, governance — before you talk to a single customer.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Product Leaders, VP of Product, Head of Customer Success, Strategy Leads

**Tags**: `cab-planning`, `strategic-framework`, `advisory-board`, `pm-tool`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
I'm establishing a Customer Advisory Board for [product/company description].

Current product stage: [early/mid/growth/mature]
Primary strategic challenge: [what we're trying to solve]
Target CAB size: [number of members]

Generate a CAB establishment framework that includes:
1. CAB mission and objectives (what success looks like in 12 months)
2. Ideal member profile (who would provide the most strategic value)
3. Member commitment expectations (time, access, format)
4. Success metrics (how we'll evaluate CAB effectiveness)
5. Operational requirements (who manages, schedules, coordinates)
6. Governance model (how decisions are made and communicated)

Be realistic about what a small team can operationalize.
A smaller, well-run CAB beats a large, chaotic one.
```

### 2. Recruitment Outreach

**Use Case**: You've identified a strategic customer. You need a personalized recruitment email that sounds like a genuine partnership invitation — not a sales pitch or reference check.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Customer Success Leads, Head of Product, Sales Engineers, Account Executives

**Tags**: `cab-recruitment`, `outreach-email`, `partnership-invite`, `customer-advisory`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
I need to recruit a customer for our Customer Advisory Board.

Customer context: [company, role, relationship history, why they're strategic]
CAB overview: [what the CAB is, why we want them specifically]

Generate:
1. Personalized recruitment email that emphasizes:
   - Strategic value of their participation
   - What they gain (market insight, early access, relationship)
   - Commitment expectations (realistically)
   - Logistical details

2. Follow-up email template if no response in 2 weeks

3. Speaking points for a live recruitment conversation

Avoid making this sound like a sales pitch or generic reference check.
Position as a genuine strategic partnership opportunity.
```

### 3. Pre-Meeting Research Synthesis

**Use Case**: You have a CAB meeting in 2 weeks. You have notes, emails, and observations about the customer. You need a 1-page briefing that tells you exactly what to ask, what to avoid, and what they'll likely say.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Product Marketing Managers, Customer Success Managers, Account Managers

**Tags**: `pre-meeting-brief`, `cab-prep`, `customer-research`, `meeting-prep`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
I'm preparing for a CAB meeting with [customer name] in [time until meeting].

Customer background:
- Company: [description]
- Their role: [role and influence level]
- Relationship history: [notable interactions]
- Past feedback: [themes from previous conversations]

I have these notes about their current situation:
[notes, emails, or observations]

Generate:
1. Pre-meeting briefing (1 page max for Product Manager reference)
2. Talking points tailored to their context
3. Questions to ask that would benefit from their specific expertise
4. Topics to avoid (sensitive areas, off-limits subjects)
5. Anticipated positions on roadmap items likely to come up

This briefing should help me make the most of limited face time.
```

### 4. Meeting Agenda Development

**Use Case**: You have 2-4 objectives for the CAB meeting. You need a structured agenda with time allocations, buffer time, and a clear flow from open to close.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Program Managers, Customer Success Leads, Event Coordinators

**Tags**: `meeting-agenda`, `cab-agenda`, `time-allocation`, `meeting-structure`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
I'm structuring a CAB meeting with these objectives:
[list 2-4 specific objectives]

Expected attendees: [list if known, or types of customers]
Meeting duration: [time]
Format: [in-person/virtual/hybrid]

Generate a meeting agenda that includes:
1. Opening frame (purpose, logistics, confidentiality norms)
2. Strategic discussion topics with time allocations
3. Product roadmap review segment (if applicable)
4. Customer concerns/open discussion time
5. Next steps and action items
6. Close with clear follow-up commitments

Build in buffer time—CAB meetings rarely run short.
Prioritize depth on fewer topics over breadth.
```

### 5. Competitive Intelligence Gathering

**Use Case**: You want to use the CAB meeting to gather competitive intel — but you don't want it to feel like a surveillance session. You need questions that sound like genuine curiosity, not competitive spying.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Competitive Intelligence, Product Marketing Managers, Strategy Leads

**Tags**: `competitive-intelligence`, `cab-questions`, `market-intel`, `competitor-analysis`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
In our upcoming CAB meeting, I want to gather competitive intelligence.

Current competitive landscape:
- [list key competitors]
- [known competitor product directions]
- [competitive threats we're tracking]

Generate a line of questioning that:
1. Surfaces what customers are seeing from competitors
2. Validates or challenges our competitive assumptions
3. Identifies emerging competitive threats we haven't considered
4. Reveals what competitors are doing well that we should learn from

Frame questions as genuine curiosity, not competitive surveillance.
Customers appreciate when you take their market perspective seriously.
```

### 6. Discussion Guide Creation

**Use Case**: You need a discussion guide for the CAB meeting. It should be a tool, not a script — structured enough to keep focus, flexible enough for organic conversation.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Facilitators, Customer Success Leads, Program Managers

**Tags**: `discussion-guide`, `cab-facilitation`, `meeting-guide`, `structured-conversation`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
I'm facilitating a CAB meeting with this focus:

Meeting theme: [primary theme or topic]
Secondary topics: [other items to cover if time allows]
Previous CAB feedback: [any unresolved items from last meeting]

Generate a discussion guide that includes:
1. Opening framing (2-3 minutes)
   - Meeting purpose
   - Confidentiality reminder
   - Format explanation

2. Primary discussion topic (30-40 minutes)
   - Opening context (what we've observed/decided)
   - Specific questions to pose
   - Hypotheses to test
   - What decision this would inform

3. Secondary topics (15-20 minutes)
   - Backup questions if primary topic resolves quickly
   - Customer-driven agenda items

4. Open forum (10-15 minutes)
   - What do customers want to discuss?
   - Emerging concerns?

5. Close (5 minutes)
   - Summarize key takeaways
   - Confirm action items
   - Next steps communication

Include facilitator notes for managing time and dynamics.
```

### 7. Difficult Conversation Preparation

**Use Case**: You need to discuss a controversial topic in the CAB — a roadmap change, a pricing shift, a feature deprecation. You need framing that acknowledges sensitivity without being defensive, plus signals for when to push vs. yield.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Product Leaders, VP of Product, Head of Customer Success

**Tags**: `difficult-conversation`, `cab-sensitive-topic`, `conflict-prep`, `framing`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
I need to discuss a controversial topic in our CAB meeting:

Topic: [what we're presenting or discussing]
Why it's sensitive: [what might generate pushback]
Our position: [what we believe is right]
Likely objections: [what objections we anticipate]

Generate:
1. Framing that acknowledges the sensitivity without being defensive
2. Data and context to share proactively
3. Questions to ask that invite dialogue rather than debate
4. Potential compromise positions or alternatives to have ready
5. Signals that indicate when to push vs. yield
6. How to close if consensus isn't possible

The goal isn't winning the argument—it's understanding their perspective
and being seen as a thoughtful partner.
```

### 8. Meeting Notes Synthesis

**Use Case**: The CAB meeting just ended. You have raw notes. You need a synthesis that separates what multiple customers said (pattern) from what one customer mentioned (individual perspective), with clear action items.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Product Marketing Managers, Customer Success Managers, Program Managers

**Tags**: `meeting-synthesis`, `cab-notes`, `action-items`, `insight-extraction`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
I've conducted a CAB meeting. Raw notes are:

[notes from the meeting]

Key participants: [who was there]
Meeting focus: [what we discussed]
Previously agreed actions: [any from last meeting]

Generate a synthesis that includes:
1. Executive summary (2-3 sentences: what we learned, so what)
2. Key insights organized by theme (bullet points)
3. Decisions made or requested (what customers committed to, what they want from us)
4. Action items with owners and timelines
5. Risks identified (things to monitor or address)
6. Relationship notes (dynamics, sentiments, follow-up needs)

Distinguish between what multiple customers said (pattern) vs.
what one customer mentioned (individual perspective).
```

### 9. Internal Briefing Generation

**Use Case**: You need to brief your internal team — product, execs, or sales — on CAB outcomes. Each audience needs a different level of detail. Executives want synthesis. Teams want actionable specifics.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Product Leaders, VP of Product, Sales Enablement, Marketing Leaders

**Tags**: `internal-briefing`, `cab-reporting`, `stakeholder-communication`, `executive-update`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
I need to brief internal stakeholders on our CAB meeting outcomes.

Audience: [product team / exec team / sales enablement]
Stakeholder concerns: [what they care about]
Meeting outcomes: [key insights and decisions]

Generate an internal briefing that:
1. Leads with the most important finding or decision
2. Provides enough context for the audience to understand implications
3. Explains what this means for our strategy or roadmap
4. Notes what we're doing in response (if decisions were made)
5. Flags anything that requires decisions or action from this audience
6. Maintains appropriate confidentiality (CAB is often confidential)

Tailor the level of detail to the audience—executives want synthesis,
teams want actionable specifics.
```

**Use Case**: The CAB meeting generated action items — both for customers and for your company. You need a tracking format with owners, timelines, status, and a customer communication plan. Nothing breeds cynicism faster than CAB feedback that disappears into a void.

**Target Personas**: Product Managers, Program Managers, Project Managers, Customer Success Leads

**Tags**: `action-tracking`, `cab-follow-through`, `accountability`, `customer-communication`, `claude`

**Prompt**:

```
Following our CAB meeting, we have these action items:

Customer action items:
- [list with owner if known]

Company action items:
- [list with owner/department]

Generate a tracking format that includes:
1. Clear action descriptions (what exactly needs to happen)
2. Owner assignment (who's accountable)
3. Timeline (when it should be complete)
4. Status (new/in-progress/complete/deferred)
5. Customer communication plan (when and how we update customers)
6. Escalation criteria (when to flag if off-track)

Track these action items religiously—nothing breeds cynicism faster
than CAB feedback that disappears into a void.
```

## ❓ FAQ (Quick Answers)

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| **How many customers should be in a CAB?** | 5-10 strategic customers. Fewer than 5 = can't represent market diversity. More than 10 = logistics eat insight. Consider a "core" CAB (5-6) + "extended" network (15-20) for specific initiatives |
| **How often should CABs meet?** | Quarterly is the minimum. High-velocity companies run bi-monthly with a subset. Annual in-person summits can replace one quarterly. Consistent cadence beats sporadic meetings |
| **Should we compensate CAB members?** | Yes — offer something. Early access, dedicated CS support, recognition, direct access to product leadership, or financial compensation. The more you ask, the more you should offer |
| **How do we handle dominant CAB members?** | Good facilitation is the first defense. Establish sharing norms in the opening. Directly invite quieter members: "Sarah, what's your perspective?" Address patterns privately. Recruit for diversity of perspectives, not just vocal ones |
| **What if CAB feedback contradicts our roadmap?** | This is where CABs earn their keep. If multiple customers say the same thing, take it seriously — even if it conflicts with your plan. Synthesize CAB input with data, strategy, and competitive landscape. Sometimes the answer is to change your plan. Sometimes it's to explain why you're not. Either way, be honest |
| **How do we prevent CAB from becoming a veto?** | CABs advise; they don't decide. This boundary must be clear from day one. When you can't act on feedback, communicate why. When you act differently, explain the reasoning. Transparency prevents CABs from becoming a customer committee |
| **Should CAB discussions be confidential?** | Yes, with rare exceptions. Members speak freely only when they trust their feedback won't be shared verbatim. Establish confidentiality norms at every meeting. Share aggregated insights externally. Direct attribution requires permission |

## 🎯 Key Takeaways (The 5 Rules)

| # | Rule |
|---|---|
| 1️⃣ | CABs are strategic, not operational. Use them for direction and market intelligence, not feature requests |
| 2️⃣ | The right participants matter most. Recruit market leaders who represent strategic segments, not just your largest accounts |
| 3️⃣ | Preparation determines outcomes. Thorough pre-meeting research and structured guides maximize limited meeting time |
| 4️⃣ | Synthesis and communication close the loop. Insights without action breed cynicism — track every action item religiously |
| 5️⃣ | Build the relationship beyond meetings. CAB value compounds with sustained engagement, consistent cadence, and visible follow-through |

> **Your CAB is only as valuable as what you do with it. Plan with intention, facilitate with structure, synthesize with rigor, and follow through without fail. 🚀**
