### 1. Industry Assumption Deconstruction Prompt

**Use Case**: Every industry runs on unquestioned assumptions — and those are exactly where disruption hides. This prompt systematically breaks those assumptions so you can see what becomes possible when they are false.

**Target Personas**: Corporate Strategists, Innovation Leads, C-Suite Executives, Business Development Heads

**Tags**: `disruptive-innovation`, `assumption-mapping`, `ai-prompts`, `strategy`, `curse-of-knowledge`

**Prompt**:

```
List the top 10 assumptions that companies in [your industry] treat as unchangeable truths. For each assumption, explain what would be possible if that assumption were false. Then identify which of these assumption violations is most likely to be enabled by current technology trends and market shifts. Finally, describe what a company that operated on this violated assumption would look like in three years.
```

### 2. Jobs-to-Be-Done Gap Analysis Prompt

**Use Case**: The best disruption opportunities are not better products — they are underserved jobs that incumbents ignore. This prompt finds those gaps and tells you what kind of non-traditional player would exploit them.

**Target Personas**: Corporate Strategists, Product Strategists, Innovation Managers, Growth Leads

**Tags**: `jobs-to-be-done`, `innovation-gaps`, `ai-prompts`, `strategy`, `competitive-analysis`

**Prompt**:

```
Analyze the [your industry] industry from the perspective of jobs-to-be-done. What are the top five jobs that customers in this space are trying to accomplish that existing solutions handle poorly? For each underserved job, explain why incumbents have not solved it despite having the resources to do so. Then identify what kind of non-traditional entrant would be motivated to solve this job differently.
```

### 3. Disruptive Scenario Stress-Test Prompt

**Use Case**: The best way to find your blind spots is to imagine a future where your core assumption gets violated — then work backward to see how much time you have. This prompt generates three specific disruption scenarios with hard timelines.

**Target Personas**: Corporate Strategists, C-Suite Executives, Board Members, Competitive Intelligence Leads

**Tags**: `scenario-planning`, `disruption-stress-test`, `ai-prompts`, `strategy`, `competitive-threats`

**Prompt**:

```
I run the strategy function at a [your industry] company. Generate three scenarios where a non-traditional entrant could capture 20% of our most profitable customer segment within three years. For each scenario: describe the new entrant's business model, identify the specific weakness in our model they would exploit, calculate how long we would have to respond before our position became untenable, and recommend the one strategic move we should make now to protect against this scenario.
```

### 4. Adjacent Industry Innovation Transfer Prompt

**Use Case**: The most disruptive ideas in your sector probably already happened in a completely different one. This prompt finds those cross-industry patterns and shows you exactly how to apply them internally.

**Target Personas**: Corporate Strategists, Innovation Leads, R&D Heads, Business Transformation Leads

**Tags**: `cross-industry-innovation`, `adjacent-markets`, `ai-prompts`, `strategy`, `ideation`

**Prompt**:

```
Identify three innovation patterns that have successfully disrupted industries adjacent to [your industry]. For each pattern, explain how it worked, what made it hard for incumbents to copy, and what would happen if that same pattern were applied to our sector. Then generate three concrete ideas for how we could apply these cross-industry innovation patterns internally.
```

### 5. Strategy Team Contrarian Challenge Prompt

**Use Case**: The most dangerous strategy meeting is the one where everyone agrees. This prompt makes AI the designated dissenter — it finds the one assumption that could kill each of your initiatives and suggests a more resilient alternative.

**Target Personas**: Corporate Strategists, Strategy Team Leads, Innovation Committees, C-Suite Executives

**Tags**: `contrarian-thinking`, `strategy-challenge`, `ai-prompts`, `assumption-testing`, `decision-quality`

**Prompt**:

```
Act as a contrarian strategist who has successfully identified three market-disrupting trends before they became obvious. Review the following strategic plan: [paste your plan]. For each major initiative, identify the one assumption that, if wrong, would cause the initiative to fail. Then rank these assumption risks by likelihood and impact. Finally, suggest one contrarian alternative to each initiative that would be more resilient to these assumption failures.
```

## ❓ FAQ

**Q: How is disruptive innovation different from regular innovation?**
A: Incremental innovation makes existing products better for existing customers. Disruptive innovation creates a new value proposition that incumbents cannot replicate because it does not compete on their terms. Disruption typically starts at the low end or in an entirely new market segment.

**Q: How do I get leadership to care about disruption when current business is strong?**
A: Use the "disruption window" framing. Current strength is the BEST time to invest — you have cash flow and credibility to experiment. Weakening companies innovate out of crisis, which is the worst time to take risks.

**Q: Is AI itself disruptive or just an efficiency tool?**
A: AI is a general-purpose technology — infrastructure for disruption, not disruption itself. The disruption comes from how AI enables new business models, not from AI as a product category.

**Q: How do you measure ROI of disruptive innovation?**
A: Traditional ROI is the wrong tool. Use a portfolio approach with stage-gate funding: small bets on many experiments, larger investments only when signals emerge. The measure of the portfolio is learning, not immediate return.

**Q: What is the biggest mistake strategists make with disruption?**
A: They confuse maintenance with innovation. Improving existing products keeps you competitive today. Challenging your own assumptions keeps you relevant tomorrow.
