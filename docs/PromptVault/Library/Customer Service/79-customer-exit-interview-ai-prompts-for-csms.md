## 🚪 Customer Exit Interview AI Prompts for CSMs

> **TL;DR**: Exit interviews reveal churn causes no other channel captures. Customers tell the truth when leaving that they won't say in surveys. Most fail because CSMs ask the wrong questions — reasons produce defensiveness, experiences produce insights. AI helps frame unbiased questions and synthesize patterns across interviews. The goal is learning, not recovery.

---

## 📝 Prompt 1. Exit Interview Preparation Brief

**Use Case**: You have a departing customer and need a full prep brief before the call — background, probe areas, land mines, and conversation framing — so you walk in ready to learn, not defend.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, Account Managers, CSM Team Leads

**Tags**: `exit-interview`, `preparation`, `churn-learning`, `conversation-framing`

**Prompt**:
```
I'm preparing for an exit interview with a departing customer.

Customer context:
- Company: [name]
- Industry: [sector]
- Their role: [title]
- Relationship history: [how long they were a customer, key milestones]
- Product usage: [what they used, how intensively]

Cancellation context:
- Stated reason (if known): [what they've said about why they're leaving]
- Timing: [renewal time / mid-term / trial conversion]
- Destination (if known): [competitor / internal solution / category exit]

Internal context:
- What we know about their experience
- Open issues or unresolved problems
- What we wish had gone differently

Generate an exit interview preparation brief that includes:
1. Key background to review before the call
2. Specific areas to probe based on this customer's context
3. Potential land mines (topics that might be sensitive)
4. What we hope to learn (specific questions this interview should answer)
5. How to frame the conversation (establish purpose and safety)

The goal is to gather insights that help us improve, not to defend our choices.
```

## 📝 Prompt 2. Interviewer Bias Prevention Framework

**Use Case**: You know your own biases will creep into the conversation. Use this to build a bias-prevention framework so you stay curious, not defensive.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, CSM Team Leads, VP of Customer Success

**Tags**: `bias-prevention`, `interviewer-bias`, `neutral-questioning`, `self-awareness`

**Prompt**:
```
I'm conducting exit interviews but want to avoid my own biases affecting the conversation.

My biases I should be aware of:
- [things I might want to hear / not want to hear]
- [defensive reactions I might have to certain feedback]
- [questions I default to because they're comfortable]

What customers typically tell me vs. what I want to know:
[gap analysis if you've noticed patterns]

Generate a bias prevention framework that includes:
1. Question framing to avoid leading questions
2. Follow-up techniques that dig deeper without being aggressive
3. How to remain neutral when hearing frustrating feedback
4. Signals that my bias might be affecting the conversation
5. Post-interview reflection questions

Good exit interviewers are curious, not defensive.
```

## 📝 Prompt 3. Opening the Exit Interview

**Use Case**: The first 60 seconds set the tone for the entire interview. Use this to craft an opening that establishes psychological safety and signals that honesty is valued over politeness.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, Account Managers, CSMs

**Tags**: `opening-framework`, `psychological-safety`, `honest-feedback`, `conversation-start`

**Prompt**:
```
I need to open an exit interview in a way that establishes psychological safety.

Customer context:
- Name: [contact name]
- Company: [name]
- Their tenure: [how long they were a customer]
- Tone of previous interactions: [what the relationship felt like]

Cancellation context:
- Stated reason: [what they said]
- Emotional tenor: [are they frustrated / neutral / apologetic about leaving?]

Generate an opening that:
1. Thanks them genuinely for their time and honesty
2. Frames the purpose as learning, not defending
3. Sets expectations for the conversation (length, topics, confidentiality)
4. Signals that honest feedback is valued more than polite feedback
5. Opens space for them to share what they really think

The opening establishes whether this will be a real conversation or a polite exit.
```

## 📝 Prompt 4. Value Discovery Questions

**Use Case**: You want to understand what value the customer ACTUALLY experienced — not what they said in surveys, but what they genuinely got (or didn't get) from your product.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, Product Managers, CSM Team Leads

**Tags**: `value-discovery`, `experience-questions`, `root-cause`, `curiosity-framing`

**Prompt**:
```
I want to understand what value the customer actually experienced during their time with us.

Customer context:
- Their role: [title]
- Company: [name]
- Use case: [what they were trying to accomplish]
- Their expectations going in: [what they hoped to get]

I want to explore:
1. What worked (what delivered on the promise)
2. What didn't work (what fell short)
3. What could have been different (what might have changed their outcome)

Generate questions that surface:
1. Specific moments of value realization (or lack thereof)
2. Barriers to achieving the value they expected
3. How their perception of value changed over time
4. Whether they would recommend based on the value they received

Frame these as curiosity, not defensiveness. You want to understand, not justify.
```

## 📝 Prompt 5. Departure Decision Exploration

**Use Case**: You know the stated reason but suspect it's not the real reason. Use this to explore the actual decision-making timeline — when they started thinking about leaving, what they compared, and what tipped the scale.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, Account Managers, CSM Team Leads

**Tags**: `departure-decision`, `decision-timeline`, `root-cause-exploration`, `alternative-evaluation`

**Prompt**:
```
I want to understand how the customer made their departure decision.

What's been shared:
- Stated reason: [what they've said]
- What I suspect vs. what they've said: [gap analysis]

I want to understand:
1. When they started considering leaving (the first signal)
2. What alternatives they evaluated
3. What ultimately tipped the decision
4. What could have changed the outcome

Generate questions that:
1. Are genuinely curious, not defensive
2. Explore timeline and process, not just conclusion
3. Help them share without feeling like they're assigning blame
4. Surface insights even if they're not directly stated

People often rationalize decisions as inevitable when they weren't.
Your job is to understand what actually drove the choice.
```

## 📝 Prompt 6. Post-Churn Outcome Questions

**Use Case**: The customer is leaving. Where are they going? Use this to explore their post-churn destination — competitor, internal build, or category exit — to understand competitive positioning and market alternatives.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, Competitive Intelligence, Product Managers

**Tags**: `post-churn`, `competitive-intelligence`, `destination-exploration`, `market-insights`

**Prompt**:
```
I want to understand what the customer is doing post-churn.

Their destination:
- [competitor / building internal solution / leaving the category]

What I want to explore:
1. What they'll use instead and why
2. What they expect to be different
3. What they're hoping to achieve that they didn't with us
4. What risks they're accepting in the change

Generate questions that:
1. Are curious about their future, not threatened by it
2. Surface what we could learn from their decision
3. Help them articulate what "better" looks like for them
4. Remain professional even if they're moving to a competitor

This information helps you understand market alternatives and competitive positioning.
```

## 📝 Prompt 7. Exploring Support and Service Failures

**Use Case**: You suspect support issues contributed to churn but the customer won't say it directly. Use this to explore their support experience without leading them — so they share frustrations safely.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, Support Leads, CSM Team Leads

**Tags**: `support-failures`, `service-exploration`, `frustration-surface`, `non-leading-questions`

**Prompt**:
```
I suspect a customer may be leaving partly due to support or service issues.

What I know:
- Their support history: [tickets, issues, resolution quality]
- Their feedback history: [any prior complaints]
- What I've observed: [patterns I noticed]

I want to understand:
1. Whether support issues contributed to their decision
2. How support interactions made them feel
3. Whether we could have saved the relationship through better support

Generate questions that:
1. Explore support experience without leading
2. Give them space to share frustrations safely
3. Help them articulate what a good support experience would have looked like
4. Don't make them feel bad about complaining

Even if I can't save this relationship, I can learn for future customers.
```

Generate questions that:
1. Explore competitive awareness without being threatened

## 📝 Prompt 10. Synthesizing a Single Exit Interview

**Use Case**: You just finished an exit interview. You have raw notes. Use this to turn them into a structured synthesis with root cause, contributing factors, and specific actionable insights — not vague "be more responsive" fluff.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, CSM Team Leads, Product Managers

**Tags**: `interview-synthesis`, `root-cause-analysis`, `actionable-insights`, `single-interview`

**Prompt**:
```
I've conducted an exit interview. Key points from the conversation:

[notes or transcript excerpts]

Context:
- Customer company: [name]
- Their role: [title]
- Stated departure reason: [what they said]
- Duration of relationship: [how long they were a customer]

Generate a synthesis that includes:
1. Primary churn cause (what actually drove the decision)
2. Contributing factors (what made it easier to leave)
3. Contributing factors on our side (what we could have done differently)
4. Protective factors that might have slowed churn
5. Could this churn have been prevented? (honest assessment)
6. Specific, actionable insights for product/implementation/success
7. What we should do differently based on this learning

Make insights specific and actionable. "Be more responsive" is not actionable.
"Response time to priority tickets averaged 48 hours—aim for 24" is.
```

## 📝 Prompt 11. Identifying Patterns Across Exit Interviews

**Use Case**: You've done multiple exit interviews. Individual notes are anecdotes. Use this to find cross-interview patterns that should drive product roadmap and process changes.

**Target Personas**: VP of Customer Success, Product Managers, CSM Team Leads, RevOps

**Tags**: `cross-exit-patterns`, `churn-themes`, `product-roadmap`, `aggregate-insights`

**Prompt**:
```
I've conducted exit interviews with [number] departing customers recently.

Summary of each interview:
[brief notes from each conversation]

Common themes emerging:
[patterns I'm already seeing]

Generate a cross-interview analysis that:
1. Identifies consistent churn drivers across multiple interviews
2. Surfaces contradictions that need explanation
3. Prioritizes issues by frequency and severity
4. Maps issues to product/feature gaps vs. relationship/process failures
5. Suggests specific actions for each insight
6. Identifies where we're hearing different things than we expected

This pattern analysis should drive product roadmap and process improvement priorities.
```

## 📝 Prompt 13. Creating Action Items from Exit Insights

**Use Case**: You have exit interview insights but no one is acting on them. Use this to turn insights into specific, owned, time-bound action items with success metrics — so learning becomes action.

**Target Personas**: Customer Success Managers, CSM Team Leads, Product Managers, Executives

**Tags**: `action-items`, `insight-to-action`, `ownership`, `success-metrics`

**Prompt**:
```
Our exit interview with [customer] revealed these key insights:

[insights from synthesis]

Company context:
- Their ARR: [revenue]
- Their strategic importance: [how important they were]
- Churn timing: [when they left]
- Competitor destination (if known): [where they went]

Generate action items that:
1. Are specific and owned by specific teams
2. Can realistically be addressed in the next quarter
3. Would have the highest impact on preventing similar churn
4. Include success metrics for each action
5. Have clear owners and timelines

Not every insight is actionable. Distinguish between:
- Things we can change (action item)
- Things we can't change but should understand (learning)
- Things that seem concerning but aren't significant (ignore)
```

## 💬 FAQ

| Question | Short Answer |
|----------|-------------|
| **Should I try to save the customer during the exit interview?** | Generally no. The goal is learning, not recovery. If you enter with a recovery agenda, you'll get polite answers, not honest ones. Exception: if you genuinely can fix their issue and they haven't fully committed. |
| **What if the customer refuses to do an exit interview?** | Respect it. Forcing it generates resentment and bad data. Use indirect signals instead: feedback history, pre-churn behavior, support/success team observations. |
| **How do I handle angry or hostile customers in exit interviews?** | Anger masks hurt. Acknowledge without defending: "I hear you're frustrated, and I appreciate you telling me directly." Don't argue. Ask questions to understand their experience. Angry data is still data. |
| **How do I avoid leading questions?** | Don't ask "Did our support fail?" Ask "What was your experience with support when issues came up?" Ask about experiences and timelines, not your hypotheses. |
| **Should I share exit interview findings with the product team?** | Yes — aggregated and anonymized. Individual feedback goes to relevant teams (e.g., support team gets support-related churn). Don't share verbatim quotes that could identify a customer. |
| **How do I know if exit interviews are representative?** | They're self-selected — some customers refuse. Track who participates vs. who doesn't. If only low-value customers do interviews, that's a bias. Supplement with other channels. |
| **What's the best way to record and store exit interview data?** | Use a standardized format: structured fields (customer, date, duration, interviewer, key quotes) + free-form observations. Store where your team can access it. Review quarterly for cross-analysis. |

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

| # | Insight |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Exit interviews are for learning, not recovery.** Trying to save the customer corrupts the data. |
| 2 | **Ask about experiences, not reasons.** Experiences surface root causes; reasons produce rationalizations. |
| 3 | **Silence is data too.** Hesitations, deflections, and topic changes reveal as much as answers. |
| 4 | **Single interviews are anecdotes; cross-exit patterns are insights.** Always synthesize across multiple interviews. |
| 5 | **Insights without action are academic exercises.** Every exit interview should drive a specific action item. |
| 6 | **Prepare before you ask.** The quality of your exit interview depends entirely on the quality of your preparation. |

## ⚠️ Common Pitfalls to Avoid

| Pitfall | Why It Hurts | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| **Trying to win back the customer during the interview** | Corrupts data — you get polite answers, not truth | Default to learning mode, not recovery mode |
| **Asking "why are you leaving?"** | Produces defensive, surface-level reasons | Ask "what was your experience when...?" instead |
| **Ignoring what's NOT said** | Hesitations and topic changes reveal more than answers | Listen for silence, deflections, and shifts |
| **Not synthesizing across interviews** | One interview = anecdote, not insight | Use Prompt 11 and 12 to find patterns |
| **Storing notes in free-form only** | Data loses value over time without structure | Use standardized format with structured + free-form fields |
| **Skipping the bias check** | Your assumptions will shape the conversation | Always run Prompt 2 before the interview |
