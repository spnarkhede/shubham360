You now see every idiom, date format, and cultural assumption as a potential market entry failure point, and you've developed an ability to spot the elements that signal "this wasn't made for us" to local customers.

# RESPONSE GUIDELINES:
This task requires three deliverables:
1. Localization Audit of Source Article
2. Adapted Article Draft
3. Localization Brief

Each deliverable must be actionable by the local market team without requiring additional clarification.

# TASK CRITERIA:
- Do not treat translation and localization as equivalent processes
- Flag culturally embedded assumptions, compliance gaps, and format dependencies
- Mark support channel assumptions and market-specific feature differences
- Remove idioms and colloquialisms completely
- Distinguish what must and must not be translated
- Focus on content that feels native, not imported

# INFORMATION ABOUT ME:
- My source article: [PASTE ARTICLE]

- My target language: [LANGUAGE]
- My market-specific product differences: [LIST ANY KNOWN DIFFERENCES, OR WRITE "NONE KNOWN"]

# RESPONSE FORMAT:
DELIVERABLE 1: LOCALIZATION AUDIT OF SOURCE ARTICLE
DELIVERABLE 2: ADAPTED ARTICLE DRAFT
DELIVERABLE 3: LOCALIZATION BRIEF FOR TRANSLATION TEAM

### Tips

You should interpret emotional nuances, identify dominant themes, cross-reference sentiment with topics, detect trend signals when time indicators are present, and provide strategic recommendations tied directly to sentiment patterns. Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step.

Analyze the provided customer feedback batch and structure your report into five distinct sections: (1) Overall Sentiment Breakdown - calculate percentage distribution across Very Positive, Positive, Neutral, Negative, and Very Negative categories with total entry count, then provide interpretive narrative explaining the overall mood beyond just restating numbers; (2) Emotion Mapping - identify dominant emotions present such as frustration, confusion, relief, gratitude, anxiety, disappointment, or surprise, noting frequency and providing 1-2 representative quotes for each emotion; (3) Sentiment by Topic - identify major topics within the feedback and cross-reference with sentiment, presenting as a matrix showing Topic | Positive | Neutral | Negative | Sample Feedback; (4) Trend Signals - note any sentiment shifts if time indicators are present in feedback (dates, "lately," "since the update," references to recent changes), or explicitly state this section is skipped if no time data exists; (5) Recommended Focus Areas - provide 2-3 specific areas for the customer service team to prioritize, with each recommendation directly tied to identified sentiment patterns.

#INFORMATION ABOUT ME:
My customer feedback data: [PASTE YOUR CUSTOMER FEEDBACK HERE]

MOST IMPORTANT!: Keep the entire report under 800 words total. Use plain language appropriate for team sharing, not academic terminology. Do not inflate analysis beyond what the data supports. If working with fewer than 50 entries, acknowledge findings are directional rather than statistically significant and present percentages as approximate. Avoid suggesting pie charts or visual elements - deliver clear, well-organized text only. Structure your response with clear section headings and present the sentiment by topic information in a simple text-based matrix format.

### What it does

- Analyzes customer feedback to identify sentiment across five categories and maps emotional patterns beyond simple positive or negative labels.

- Generates actionable recommendations for customer service teams based on sentiment patterns and emotional insights found in the AI prompt analysis.

### Tips

- Review your customer feedback data before running this AI prompt to ensure it includes enough variety and volume, which helps the sentiment analysis deliver meaningful patterns rather than surface-level observations.
- Use the sentiment report output to train your customer service team on emotional intelligence, focusing on the emotion mapping section to help them recognize and respond to feelings like frustration or anxiety in real-time interactions.
- After generating the initial report, ask the AI prompt to compare sentiment patterns across different time periods or customer segments, revealing deeper insights about how specific groups or moments drive your overall customer experience trends.

Each answer must follow the inverted pyramid structure—leading with the direct solution in the first sentence, then adding context or steps only if needed. Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step.

Begin by analyzing the recurring issues provided and grouping them into logical FAQ categories such as Billing & Payments, Account Setup, Product Features, or Troubleshooting. Organize categories by how customers think about problems, not how your internal team structures them. For multi-step processes, use numbered lists with no more than 7 steps; if more steps are required, break the process into logical sub-sections. Eliminate walls of unformatted text and passive-voice constructions that bury solutions. Use plain, direct language at an 8th-grade reading level to maximize accessibility and comprehension.
